H-Neurons:
Replication, Artifacts,
and Open Questions

Gao et al. (2025) claim that fewer than 0.1% of a language model's MLP neurons, dubbed "H-Neurons," reliably predict hallucination and causally drive over-compliance when amplified. We replicated their pipeline on Gemma 3 4B-IT and confirmed the core signal: 38 neurons out of 348,160 predict hallucination at 76.5% accuracy on fully held-out data, and scaling them monotonically increases compliance with misleading context. But replication also surfaced problems the paper doesn't discuss.

The top-ranked neuron's classifier weight is an L1 regularization artifact, not a uniquely important "hallucination hub." Only 14% of evaluation samples actually respond to the intervention; the 6.3pp headline effect is really a 45.7% swing concentrated in a small sensitive subpopulation. The paper's standard-prompt evaluation also likely underreports compliance at high α because the model emits exact answer text instead of option letters, which MC-letter parsers misread as non-compliance. Strict text remapping at α=3.0 lifts the raw 63.6% to 72.1%, restoring the expected monotonic trend. Negative controls confirm the effect is H-neuron-specific: scaling 38 random neurons produces a compliance slope 26× flatter.

The replication holds. Three extensions are now running: SAE decomposition to find a monosemantic "compliance feature" inside the polysemantic H-Neurons, using Gemma Scope's 16k-width SAEs that fit alongside the model in 16GB VRAM; anti-H-Neuron investigation of the 38 negative-weight (hallucination-suppressive) neurons the paper ignored, testing whether amplifying a natural truthfulness circuit can reduce hallucination; and swing sample profiling to determine whether H-Neurons override genuine knowledge (alarming) or just mask appropriate uncertainty (a narrower safety concern).

Questions for advisor

Two strategy decisions to make before the next wave of runs

These should be settled before more compute gets spent. They decide which detector counts as the main object of study and which second model buys the most signal per unit of effort.

1. Which neuron set should define the main story?

Should the project stay anchored to the 38 neurons at C=1.0 because that is closest to the paper's sparse-intervention framing, or pivot to the 219 positive neurons at C=3.0 because that detector appears to capture more of the real Gemma signal?

The tradeoff is straightforward: C=1.0 buys paper faithfulness and a clean replication headline, while C=3.0 buys better predictive performance and a story less vulnerable to the 4288 artifact.

2. Which second model adds the most value?

If the replication extends beyond Gemma-3-4B, should the next target be Mistral-Small-24B or Llama-3.3-70B?

Mistral is the cleaner engineering continuation and sits close to the paper's best-behaving family. Llama is the stronger external-validity test, but it asks for a much more expensive deployment story.

Next steps

Three highest-signal follow-ups

The first closes the evaluator gap across the full sweep. The other two test whether the effect is specific and what mechanism is actually moving.

Extend text-based scoring (~30–60 min, $0)

Apply the same answer-text remap used at α=3.0 across the full standard-prompt sweep, then recompute the curve and the population split from that corrected series.

Negative control (~1 h, $0)

Scale 38 random non-H-neurons by the same α values. This is the cleanest test of whether the observed behavior change is specific or just generic activation damage.

Prompt disentangling (~1–2 h, $0)

Run a no-context standard prompt and an aligned-format prompt so answer selection, answer rendering, and generic degradation can be separated more cleanly.
