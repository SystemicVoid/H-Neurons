Process note

The funnel shape tells a story about methodological choices masquerading as data. The 89% consistency filter is aggressive — it discards every question where the model hesitates even once across 10 samples. This selects for questions the model either knows cold or consistently gets wrong, filtering out the messy middle where hallucination is most interesting. The 118 apostrophe failures (3.8%) are not random either — they systematically remove Irish names, possessives, and contractions. Every pipeline stage shapes what the classifier can learn.

On the data leakage

Finding 66.3% train/test overlap was the first surprise. The paper samples train and test independently, which is standard — but with only 1,391 true-label IDs and both splits requesting 1,000, overlap is mathematically inevitable. The reassuring part: the accuracy drop from 77.7% to 76.5% is only 1.2 percentage points, meaning the leakage inflated results modestly, not catastrophically. The signal survived a clean test. This is worth flagging as a general replication concern — papers that don't report overlap statistics may be hiding this kind of quiet inflation.

What this means: Hallucination detection is not a last-layer phenomenon. The neurons that distinguish true from false activations are distributed across the model, with a strong bias toward early layers — consistent with early commitment to an answer, though other interpretations are possible.
What surprised us

Layer 5 alone hosts 4 of 38 H-Neurons — more than any other layer. In a 34-layer model, the "decision to hallucinate" appearing this early is counterintuitive. One interpretation: early MLP layers encode token familiarity — how well the model "recognizes" the entity before deep reasoning begins. A question about O'Neill vs Shakespeare might already register different confidence patterns in layer 5, before attention has had a chance to compose multi-hop reasoning. This aligns with recent residual stream probing work showing factual recall signals emerging in the first third of transformer depth.

The surprise

The question: L1 regularization has a known "winner-take-all" effect — when two features carry similar signals, L1 arbitrarily assigns all weight to one and zeros the other. A 1.65x gap is unusual enough to warrant investigation. We ran six independent analyses to determine the truth.
Forensic investigation

Six tests all point to a regularization artifact

Each analysis probes neuron 4288's importance from a different angle. Real signal requires at least 4 of 6 supporting evidence.

1. Single-neuron AUC Artifact

Can neuron 4288 alone classify hallucinations? Its standalone AUC is 0.590 — outperformed by L13:N833 (0.703) and L14:N8547 (0.666). Despite the highest L1 weight, it's not the most informative individual neuron.

2. Distribution separation Artifact

Cohen's d = 0.326 (below 0.5 threshold). The runner-up achieves d = 0.477 with better class separation. Neuron 4288 has signal, but it's not exceptional.

3. C-sweep stability Artifact

The most decisive test. Neuron 4288 is completely absent at C ≤ 0.3. It only enters at C = 1.0, drops to rank 5 at C = 3.0, and rank 11 at C = 10.0. Its dominance is C-specific, not fundamental.

4. Per-example contribution Artifact

Despite 1.65x the weight of #2, neuron 4288 is the largest positive contributor for only 7.4% of examples (threshold: 30%). Median absolute contribution: just 2.6% of total score.

5. Leave-one-out ablation Artifact

Zeroing out neuron 4288 drops accuracy by 1.03pp (below 2pp threshold). Keeping only 4288 yields AUC = 0.828 but accuracy = 50.1% — good ranking, no decision power alone.

6. Correlation structure Artifact

Neuron 4288 correlates at r = 0.492 with L26:N1359 (a zero-weight neuron). L1 likely picked 4288 as the "representative" of a correlated feature group.

Methodological reflection

The investigation of neuron 4288 wasn't planned — it emerged from a simple question: "why is one neuron 1.65x the runner-up?" The six-test framework was designed to be unfalsifiable from any single angle. We set thresholds before running the analyses (AUC>0.60, Cohen's d>0.5, ablation>2pp) to avoid post-hoc rationalization. The unanimous 0/6 verdict was the strongest possible outcome — and it points to a systematic issue with L1-based neuron ranking that likely affects other work using this methodology. The C-sweep (Analysis 3) was the most revealing: the fact that C=3.0 yields +4pp accuracy with 6x more neurons suggests the paper's headline "~35 H-Neurons" is a regularization artifact, not a fundamental property of the model.

The open question is why higher α preserves answer content while shifting the surface form.
Open questions

Three mechanisms we cannot yet distinguish

The data rules out a simple "general obedience" story. Here are three competing explanations for why α boosts content-following while making standard-prompt outputs harder for the MC-letter evaluator to parse.

A

Context-credulity neurons

These neurons modulate how much the model treats provided context as ground truth. High α makes it more credulous (accept counterfactual claims) while reducing attention to evaluator-friendly answer framing. The surface-form shift is a side effect of over-weighting context.

Test it: Run without any context passage. If high α still shifts outputs away from recoverable letters, credulity is not the mechanism.

B

Answer-text bypass

These neurons may amplify answer selection more than answer rendering. The model picks the context-supported option more often, but emits the option text rather than the MC label, which hurts letter-based evaluation without necessarily hurting prompt compliance.

Test it: Re-score the 150 unparseable α=3.0 responses by matching answer text to option text. If most map cleanly to the counterfactual option, this bypass story gains support.

C

Generic surface-form degradation

Extreme activation scaling (α=2–3) simply degrades output precision. Exact-answer strings and short free-form responses survive better than precise symbolic formatting, so letter-based evaluation breaks before semantic answer content does.

Test it: Scale 38 random non-H-neurons by the same α values. If parsability degrades similarly, the effect is not specific to these neurons.

We don't know yet. The data is consistent with all three mechanisms. The negative control is still the most urgent, but the cheapest missing analysis is even simpler: re-score the unparseable standard-prompt answers by text rather than by letter.

On negative controls

Neither the paper nor this replication tests whether scaling 38 random non-H-neurons by the same α values would produce a similar compliance trend. Without this negative control, we can't rule out that any perturbation to intermediate activations increases compliance. This is the single most valuable experiment still missing — it would take ~2 hours and $0 to run.

Takeaways

Four things we now know (and don't know)

1

The signal is real — and causal on FaithEval

76.5% detection accuracy on a disjoint test set. On FaithEval, content-level compliance rises with α under both prompts; the standard raw decline is explained largely by an MC-letter parsing confound.

2

Broader than hallucination — but not a simple obedience dial

The data is consistent with a context-following role, but the standard prompt shows a split between answer content and surface form. That is broader than hallucination detection, but still not a clean "do what you're told" mechanism.

3

Effect is concentrated

80–86% of samples are unaffected by α. The headline effect is diluted across a frozen majority. The causal influence acts on the uncertain margin, not on deeply-held beliefs or obvious fabrications.

4

Prompt engineering dwarfs it

Our slope (2.10%/α) means a full α=0→3 sweep yields 6.3pp. Prompt engineering routinely achieves 30–50pp swings. Neuron scaling establishes causation but is a weak practical lever.

The bigger picture

We went from correlation to causation, and from causation to confusion — which is progress. The paper frames these as "hallucination neurons." Our first prompt variant suggested "compliance neurons." The standard-prompt rerun forced a tighter claim: these neurons increase content-following under misleading context, but they also shift outputs away from the MC-letter surface form our evaluator expects. That means even "compliance" is too simple. The honest answer is that we don't yet know what these neurons do, but the uncertainty is now better localized: content selection, answer rendering, or generic surface-form degradation.

Questions for advisor

Two strategy decisions to make before the next wave of runs

These belong before the execution plan. They decide which neuron set counts as the main object of study, and which second model would add the most external validity per unit of compute.

1. Which neuron set should define the main story?

Should we keep focusing on the 38 neurons at C=1.0 because that is closest to the paper's sparse-intervention methodology, or pivot to the 219 positive neurons at C=3.0 because that detector appears to capture more of the real Gemma signal?

Current evidence: C=1.0 gives the paper-faithful sparse story and reproduced the paper's Gemma-3-4B TriviaQA accuracy almost exactly (paper 76.9%, ours 76.5% disjoint). But our C-sweep found C=3.0 reaches 80.5% accuracy with 219 positive neurons, and neuron 4288 drops from rank 1 to rank 5, which suggests the 38-neuron set is partly a regularization artifact rather than the full circuit.

2. Which second model adds the most value?

If we replicate beyond Gemma-3-4B, should the next target be Mistral-Small-24B or Llama-3.3-70B?

Mentor framing: Mistral is the cleaner engineering choice; Llama is the stronger external-validity choice. Mistral sits on the paper's best-behaving family and is feasible on our validated ~96GB single-GPU path. Llama-3.3-70B posts the strongest large-model paper numbers, but our current hardware notes say a pure-GPU bf16 run does not fit on a single GH200, so reproducing it would require a more expensive deployment strategy.

Next steps

Three highest-signal follow-ups

The first resolves today's measurement confound. The other two test whether the effect is specific and what mechanism is most plausible.

Re-score parse failures (~20min, $0)

Map the 150 unparseable standard-prompt responses at α=3.0 back to answer-option text. This directly tests whether the raw drop is mostly an evaluator artifact or a true loss of content-following.

Negative control (~1h, $0)

Scale 38 random non-H-neurons by the same α values. Tests Hypothesis C (generic degradation). If content and parsability shift similarly for random neurons, the effect is not specific to H-Neurons. The single most important missing experiment.

Prompt disentangling (~1–2h, $0)

Run two small ablations: a no-context version of the standard prompt, and an aligned-instruction prompt where answer selection and output format point the same way. Together they separate context-credulity from answer-rendering effects.
