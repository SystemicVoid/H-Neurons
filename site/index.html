Key findings

86% of the population is frozen. The real effect is 10x larger than the headline.

The no-op-to-max effect is +4.5pp [2.9, 6.1] (full sweep 6.3pp). But 60% of samples always comply and 26% never do regardless of scaling. The entire effect is driven by 138 swing samples (13.8%), where H-neuron amplification overrides prior knowledge in 68.1% of cases. The headline number is diluted by frozen populations the paper does not separate.

Population breakdown

The paper's #1 neuron is an L1 regularization artifact

Neuron (20, 4288) has 1.65x the classifier weight of the runner-up but scores 0/6 on independent importance tests. The real best single predictor is L13:N833 (AUC 0.703 vs 0.590). At C=3.0 the signal spreads across 219 neurons and accuracy rises to 80.5%. L1 weight is not a reliable proxy for neuron importance.

Six-test investigation

H-neuron specificity confirmed with per-alpha negative controls the paper never ran

The original paper compares H-neuron vs random-neuron detection classifiers but runs no random-neuron control on the intervention experiments. We ran 8 random-neuron sets (38 neurons each) across every alpha on FaithEval and 3 on FalseQA. Random slopes: [-0.11, 0.16] pp/α. H-neuron slope: 2.09 pp/α. The effect is specific to these neurons, not a generic perturbation artifact.

Negative control data

Binary jailbreak judges miss the real safety story. Graded judging recovers it.

The original 256-token jailbreak slope (+6.2 pp) was a truncation artifact — the model was cut off mid-disclaimer, creating alpha-dependent false positives. A 5000-token rerun produced a non-significant binary slope (+3.0 pp, CI includes zero). But CSV-v2 graded judging separates genuine compliance (C≥2) from disclaimer-wrapped discussion (C=1, constant ~60-response noise floor). The real effect: V=3 polished harmful content nearly quadruples (3.8%→14.0%), payload share rises from 21%→26%, and pivot position recedes from 16%→10% of response length. 76% of the count increase is ablation recovery (α=0→1), not amplification.

Refusal-direction ablation has a cliff, not a curve

All-layer refusal-direction ablation on FaithEval has one usable point (β=0.02, +4.2 pp) before a catastrophic cliff at β=0.03 (−14.9 pp). The collapse is not a parser failure — it is answer-option bias: output B jumps from 28% to 58%, while C and D compliance drops to 22% and 29%. For MCQ steering evaluations, output-distribution checks matter more than parse-failure rates.

SAE features detect hallucination but cannot steer it

An SAE probe matches neuron-level detection (AUROC 0.848 vs 0.843), but both full-replacement and delta-only steering produce zero compliance slope (0.16 and 0.12 pp/α). Features that correlate with a behavior in static activations do not necessarily causally control it. This detection-steering dissociation is novel.

SAE closed-out details

Same 38 neurons shift behavior across 3 different benchmarks

FaithEval (+4.5 pp above no-op; slope +2.09 pp/α) and FalseQA (slope +1.62 pp/α) show compliance increasing with H-neuron scaling, both with independent negative controls confirming specificity. Jailbreak binary compliance is non-significant (+3.0 pp, CI includes zero), but CSV-v2 graded judging reveals a monotonic severity escalation: the intervention does not create more jailbreaks, it makes existing ones structurally worse. 0.011% of the network, consistent causal role across qualitatively different tasks.

Cross-benchmark results

Detection signal survives a truly disjoint test

The paper's TriviaQA evaluation had 66.3% train/test overlap (independently sampled splits from small pools). We built a 0%-overlap disjoint test set. Accuracy: 76.5% [73.6, 79.5], actually closer to the paper's 76.9% than the inflated 77.7% from the overlapping split. The signal is real, not leakage.
