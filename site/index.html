Key findings

86% of the population is frozen. The real effect is 10x larger than the headline.

The paper reports a 6.3pp compliance swing. But 60% of samples always comply and 26% never do regardless of scaling. The entire effect is driven by 138 swing samples (13.8%), where H-neuron amplification overrides prior knowledge in 68.1% of cases. The headline number is diluted by frozen populations the paper does not separate.

Population breakdown

The paper's #1 neuron is an L1 regularization artifact

Neuron (20, 4288) has 1.65x the classifier weight of the runner-up but scores 0/6 on independent importance tests. The real best single predictor is L13:N833 (AUC 0.703 vs 0.590). At C=3.0 the signal spreads across 219 neurons and accuracy rises to 80.5%. L1 weight is not a reliable proxy for neuron importance.

Six-test investigation

H-neuron specificity confirmed with per-alpha negative controls the paper never ran

The original paper compares H-neuron vs random-neuron detection classifiers but runs no random-neuron control on the intervention experiments. We ran 8 random-neuron sets (38 neurons each) across every alpha on FaithEval and 3 on FalseQA. Random slopes: [-0.11, 0.16] pp/α. H-neuron slope: 2.09 pp/α. The effect is specific to these neurons, not a generic perturbation artifact.

Negative control data

SAE features detect hallucination but cannot steer it

An SAE probe matches neuron-level detection (AUROC 0.848 vs 0.843), but both full-replacement and delta-only steering produce zero compliance slope (0.16 and 0.12 pp/α). Features that correlate with a behavior in static activations do not necessarily causally control it. This detection-steering dissociation is novel.

SAE closed-out details

Same 38 neurons shift behavior across 3 different benchmarks

FaithEval (+6.3pp), FalseQA (+4.8pp), and jailbreak (+6.2pp) all show compliance increasing with H-neuron scaling. Two of three have independent negative controls confirming specificity. 0.011% of the network, consistent causal role across qualitatively different tasks and evaluators.

Cross-benchmark results

Detection signal survives a truly disjoint test

The paper's TriviaQA evaluation had 66.3% train/test overlap (independently sampled splits from small pools). We built a 0%-overlap disjoint test set. Accuracy: 76.5% [73.6, 79.5], actually closer to the paper's 76.9% than the inflated 77.7% from the overlapping split. The signal is real, not leakage.
