# **Claim-Boundary Audit and Literature Map: Steering Targets in Mechanistic Interpretability**

## **1\. Executive Novelty Verdict**

A rigorous assessment of the contemporary mechanistic interpretability and representation engineering literature—extending through early 2026—indicates that the foundational premise of the proposed research is epistemologically sound but highly vulnerable to overclaiming if framed incorrectly. The assertion that high probe accuracy, decodability, or feature detectability does not inherently guarantee causal utility is firmly established background knowledge. Modern literature has moved significantly beyond the classic "probing versus causality" debates of the early 2020s. Recent empirical studies explicitly document a "correlation/causation dissociation" within state-of-the-art architectures, demonstrating that predictive vectors in latent space can possess near-perfect correlation coefficients while yielding zero causal efficacy for behavioral steering.1 Consequently, constructing a flagship methods paper around the broad slogan that "detectors fail as steering targets" presents an unacceptable risk; such a claim will be categorized by reviewers as a delayed replication of existing critiques regarding linear probing and feature geometry.

However, a highly defensible and genuinely novel frontier exists in the exact empirical and conceptual form proposed by the project. The true novelty lies not in the mere existence of a decodability-causality gap, but in the systematic demonstration that predictive readout quality is an unreliable heuristic for steering-target selection, and critically, that this unreliability cascades predictably across mismatched evaluation surfaces. The literature currently lacks an integrated, four-stage analytical scaffold—comprising Measurement, Localization, Control, and Externality—as a formalized mechanism to diagnose activation steering failures.

By systematically charting how matched detection signals between distinct architectural objects (such as Sparse Autoencoder features versus hallucination-associated neurons) diverge entirely at the control stage, and how functional control subsequently shatters at the externality stage when transferring from multiple-choice formats to open-ended generation, the proposed paper transitions from a narrow critique of probing to a comprehensive, systemic audit of representation engineering pipelines. The flagship paper must therefore abandon any attempt to claim that detectors never work. Instead, it must assert that the structural conflation of measurement, localization, control, and externality is the primary driver of illusory steering results, utilizing Gemma-3-4B-IT 2 as the highly controlled, multimodal-capable testbed to map these exact failure boundaries. The most defensible posture acknowledges that while some detector-selected targets successfully steer single-axis dispositions, the heuristic itself is fundamentally untrustworthy for complex factual and alignment interventions without multi-stage validation.

## **3\. Paper-Safe Evidence Hierarchy**

Navigating the contemporary literature requires distinguishing between precedents that actively threaten the core novelty of the paper versus those that merely affect the supporting edge or caveated arguments. The most significant threat to the un-caveated version of the paper's thesis is the 2026 work on behavioral steering in large Mixture-of-Experts models.1 This work explicitly identifies a "correlation/causation dissociation," demonstrating that a risk-calibration probe with an ![][image2] of 0.795 fails entirely to steer behavior, while a deference probe with a lower ![][image2] of 0.737 succeeds. This directly establishes that high probe accuracy does not guarantee causal relevance in the context of modern SAE feature steering. If the flagship paper rests solely on the observation that a good detector can be a bad steering target, it will be subsumed by this precedent.

Conversely, literature surrounding hallucination-associated neurons (H-neurons) poses a threat only to the localization novelty, not the integrated thesis. Research demonstrates that a remarkably sparse subset of neurons—less than 0.1 percent—can reliably predict hallucination and that these neurons are causally linked to over-compliance behaviors.3 While this establishes that H-neurons are potent, causally relevant targets, it does not address the comparative operator mismatch. The flagship paper maintains its safe core by leveraging this established H-neuron potency against SAE feature steering, testing them head-to-head on the FaithEval diagnostic surface. The existing literature maps these concepts in parallel, but never intersecting; the intersection is the safe harbor for the flagship's experimental contribution.

The literature regarding the "knowledge-prediction gap" represents a moderate threat to the externality novelty. Recent studies establish that models frequently fail multiple-choice questions despite possessing the correct knowledge perfectly encoded in their residual streams, accessible via simple linear operations.4 This explains why multiple-choice surfaces are flawed, but it operates purely in an observational probing regime. The flagship paper elevates this by transitioning from observation to active intervention, demonstrating how steering to fix a multiple-choice gap actively induces collateral damage—such as confident wrong-entity substitution—on open-ended generation benchmarks like BRIDGE. Therefore, the knowledge-prediction gap literature should be utilized as foundational scaffolding rather than viewed as a novelty threat.

## **4\. The Four-Stage Prior-Work Map**

The literature mapping must be structured identically to the paper's proposed four-stage scaffold. This approach not only organizes the prior art but explicitly tests whether the integrated framing already exists. The analysis confirms that while individual stages are heavily researched, the synthesis of Measurement, Localization, Control, and Externality as separable empirical dependencies remains absent.

### **4.1 Measurement: Evaluator and Metric Artifacts**

The measurement stage questions whether the evaluation surface accurately reflects the underlying model capability or behavior. In recent months, the literature has identified severe vulnerabilities in LLM evaluation protocols that routinely distort scientific conclusions. Judge dependence is a primary vector of failure. The advent of tools like the J-detector highlights that automated LLM-as-a-judge systems produce systematic scoring artifacts, relying heavily on superficial linguistic features rather than genuine semantic compliance.6 If an activation steering intervention is optimized to satisfy an LLM judge, the intervention may simply be forcing the model into an artifact-rich subspace, creating an illusion of successful control.

Furthermore, truncation artifacts and context saturation critically undermine safety and compliance evaluations. Evaluations of frontier models, such as Gemini 2.5 Flash, demonstrate that under high intrinsic load or specific sequence lengths, accuracy and compliance can plummet to near zero.7 If a steered model produces a shorter response that naturally evades context saturation, an automated evaluator might score the intervention as a success, masking the fact that the steering target simply damaged the model's generative stamina. Finally, the shift from binary to graded scoring fundamentally alters the interpretation of intervention success. Binary detection provides a convenient benchmark but fails to capture whether a model truly understands the nature of a vulnerability, often leading to false positives in safety steering evaluations.8

### **4.2 Localization: Probes, SAEs, and H-Neurons**

Localization attempts to isolate the specific neural substrate where a feature or readout signal resides. The literature is dense with methodologies designed to pinpoint these functional units, heavily dominated by two distinct lineages: Sparse Autoencoders and specific neuron heuristics. SAEs decompose dense activations into sparse, human-interpretable features. However, recent scaling efforts applied to massive hybrid architectures reveal that the localized feature geometry is highly complex. Projecting probe weights through an SAE's decoder bypasses top-k discretization, but the resulting localized vectors often map to non-causal correlates.1

Concurrently, heuristic neuron targeting has advanced significantly. Hallucination-associated neurons (H-neurons) have been identified via magnitude analysis as a highly sparse, potent subset that emerges inherently during the pre-training objective, rather than being an artifact of supervised fine-tuning or reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF).3 The literature treats localization via SAEs and localization via H-neurons as equally valid diagnostic steps to narrow the search space 9, yet it fails to systematically compare what occurs when these two localized signals are subjected to identical intervention operators.

### **4.3 Control: The Correlation-Causation Dissociation**

Control examines whether intervening at the localized site reliably and predictably steers behavior. The literature explicitly demonstrates that precise localization is a necessary but deeply insufficient condition for control. The most robust precedent for this control break is found in the analysis of 35B parameter Mixture-of-Experts models. Researchers extracted SAE-decoded probe vectors and found that while multiple vectors exhibited matched decodability (correlation coefficients near 0.79), their causal efficacy was entirely divergent. Intervening on one vector merely suppressed outputs, while intervening on a geometrically orthogonal vector successfully steered the model's disposition.1

Additionally, temporal mechanics within the forward pass restrict control. Studies indicate that behavioral commitments are often "compiled" during the prefill phase in certain architectures. Consequently, activation steering applied exclusively during autoregressive decoding yields a null effect, completely breaking the control mechanism despite perfect localization.1 Control is thus highly sensitive to both the geometric properties of the localized vector and the temporal application of the intervention operator.

### **4.4 Externality: Surface Mismatch and Collateral Damage**

Externality evaluates whether achieved control transfers seamlessly across differing tasks without inducing catastrophic side effects or localized overfitting. The literature has begun to document severe breaks between highly constrained evaluation surfaces and open-ended generation. Inference-Time Intervention (ITI) serves as the canonical example. Early ITI implementations shifted activations along truthful directions, resulting in a dramatic, 30+ point increase in generative truthfulness. However, the corresponding improvement in multiple-choice accuracy on the exact same dataset was statistically marginal.5 This discrepancy arises because multiple-choice accuracy relies on the model's internal probability distribution over pre-defined candidate strings, whereas generative truthfulness alters the physical autoregressive path.

Externality failures are particularly acute in open-ended factual generation. When control is optimized to satisfy a narrow benchmark, the model frequently collapses into safe but completely unhelpful states, heavily favoring disclaimer-laden outputs.5 In more complex retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) environments, such as those modeled by the BRIDGE benchmark, unaddressed holes in contextual relevance heavily distort the alignment between retrieval and generation.10 Steering targets selected via narrow heuristics actively induce negative externalities, destroying the model's broader factual grounding and leading to confident entity substitutions when evaluated outside the optimized surface.

## **5\. Anchor One: The Localization to Control Break**

The first anchor of the flagship paper investigates the specific disconnect where matched detection quality results in completely divergent steering outcomes. To ensure claim hygiene, this must be meticulously contextualized against the current literature. The flagship paper relies on a cross-operator comparison, testing SAE features against H-neurons on the FaithEval diagnostic surface within the Gemma-3-4B-IT architecture.

FaithEval provides the ideal, highly controlled diagnostic environment. Comprising nearly 5,000 high-quality problems, it forces the language model to navigate counterfactual, inconsistent, and unanswerable contexts. The benchmark is specifically designed to assess whether a model remains faithful to the provided context or defaults to its internal parametric recall.11 For instance, in the unanswerable subset, the answer-bearing sentence is removed, and the model must successfully refuse to answer rather than hallucinate a response based on prior knowledge. This directly targets the mechanism of over-compliance and confabulation.

The literature establishes H-neurons as a raw, architectural mechanism causally linked to this exact type of over-compliance. Suppressing H-neurons reliably reduces hallucinations without broadly degrading overall model fluency.3 Conversely, steering via SAE features involves extracting a linear difference-in-means direction or training a linear probe on the SAE's latent activations, then projecting those weights back into the native activation space.1

The critical novelty of the flagship paper is demonstrating that if an SAE feature and an H-neuron are selected such that they possess identical decodability or readout quality for a specific FaithEval context, their steering efficacy will still diverge. The literature currently tests these operators in isolation. By testing them head-to-head, the flagship paper establishes that the intervention operator itself interacts unpredictably with the localized representation. This proves that standard target-selection heuristics, which rely solely on detection quality, are systematically flawed because they ignore the geometric and architectural mismatch between the localized feature and the applied operator.

## **6\. Anchor Two: The Control to Externality Break**

The second anchor addresses the phenomenon of surface-local control, examining why an intervention that seemingly succeeds on one evaluation surface fails, backfires, or becomes entirely non-transferable on another. The literature provides critical foundational evidence for this break, primarily through the lens of TruthfulQA and the dichotomy between multiple-choice (MC) and generative evaluations.

Inference-Time Intervention (ITI) studies explicitly highlight this surface mismatch. As noted, shifting activations along truth-correlated directions radically improves autoregressive generation scores while leaving multiple-choice accuracy largely stagnant.5 This is fundamentally tied to the "knowledge-prediction gap," a widespread phenomenon across MCQ benchmarks where models encode the correct ground-truth answer in their hidden representations—perfectly accessible via a linear knowledge probe—yet ultimately predict the incorrect option. This gap demonstrates a severe geometric misalignment between the model's internal knowledge subspace and its output prediction subspace.4

The flagship paper extends this foundational observation by analyzing the severe externalities of entity substitution on open-ended factual generation. When researchers force control via activation steering to artificially close this knowledge-prediction gap on a multiple-choice benchmark, the intervention frequently overfits to the local surface geometry. Consequently, when the model is transitioned to a complex, multi-hop open-ended generation task—such as those evaluated in the BRIDGE benchmark—the brittle intervention collapses. The BRIDGE benchmark, built to refine Information Retrieval (IR) evaluations by uncovering missing relevant chunks, illustrates how retrieval-generation misalignment occurs.10 The flagship paper will demonstrate that benchmark-local steering targets actively induce negative externalities in these complex generative environments, causing the model to confidently substitute incorrect entities because the steering vector disrupted the broader semantic network required for open-ended factual coherence.

## **7\. Anchor Three: The Measurement to Conclusion Break**

The final anchor challenges the epistemological bedrock of representation engineering: the integrity of the measurement surface itself. The literature increasingly indicates that evaluator choice, metric design, and parser assumptions can entirely reverse the scientific conclusion regarding the efficacy of safety interventions and activation steering.

Judge dependence is a critical point of failure. The widespread adoption of LLM-as-a-judge frameworks introduces deep, systematic evaluation artifacts. Tools such as the J-detector explicitly capture the correlations between response characteristics and assigned scores, revealing that automated judges reliably reward specific linguistic markers, formatting choices, and disclaimer-heavy outputs rather than genuine behavioral compliance or safety.6 Consequently, an activation steering vector that appears to successfully align a model may merely be steering the model's output into the linguistic subspace favored by the automated judge.

Truncation artifacts pose an equally severe threat to measurement validity. Recent stress tests of frontier models, including Gemini 2.5 Flash, reveal that accuracy and safety compliance can completely degrade due to context saturation and sequence length, completely independent of the model's internal capability.7 If a steered model produces a truncated response that avoids hitting the context saturation threshold, an evaluator might erroneously score the intervention as a success, masking the reality that the steering target merely damaged the model's generative stamina.

Furthermore, the transition from binary to graded scoring alters intervention verdicts. Binary detection is a convenient heuristic but fails to capture the nuanced nature of vulnerabilities or partial compliance, often resulting in superficial safety ratings.8 The companion measurement note will rigorously document how an intervention in Gemma-3-4B-IT that achieves near-perfect success under a binary harmfulness classifier completely fails when subjected to a graded, multi-dimensional evaluation of compliance, proving that the measurement choice directly dictates the scientific conclusion.

## **8\. Positive Counterexamples and Boundary Conditions**

A rigorous claim-boundary audit requires explicitly acknowledging where detector-selected or probe-selected targets *do* steer well. By defining these boundary conditions, the flagship paper immunizes itself against accusations of selective reporting and strengthens its core argument that readouts are unreliable heuristics, rather than universally broken mechanisms.

The literature identifies three primary domains where detection reliably translates to control. First, steering targets selected via probes are highly effective when modulating single, dominant dispositional axes. For example, steering the "autonomy versus deference" axis in a 35B MoE model achieved a massive behavioral shift, with a Cohen's ![][image3] of 1.01, indicating that broad, systemic behavioral states are amenable to heuristic target selection.1 Second, refusal behaviors are frequently mediated by a single, robust geometric direction; steering along this single vector effectively and reliably toggles the model's compliance constraints without complex externalities.12 Third, targeted semantic edits utilizing sparse, orthogonal constraints—such as the MAT-STEER framework—successfully modulate specific attributes like truthfulness or toxicity without inducing severe attribute conflicts, achieving high win rates against standard fine-tuning baselines.13

These counterexamples establish the necessary limits of the flagship claim. Detector-selected targets are structurally unreliable specifically for complex, multi-faceted tasks—such as multi-hop factual generation or nuanced knowledge retrieval—where the localized feature must interact non-linearly with multiple computational pathways. When the behavioral target is a simple, pervasive disposition, the heuristic holds.

## **10\. Reviewer-Objection Memo**

A skeptical reviewer in the mechanistic interpretability domain will inevitably raise structural objections to the integrated four-stage claim. The following arguments synthesize the literature to pre-emptively neutralize these specific critiques.

**Objection 1: "This is just old decodability-vs-causality."**

*The Rebuttal:* The paper explicitly acknowledges the decodability-causality gap as established background. The core novelty is not the mere existence of the gap, but the systemic demonstration that modern representation engineering pipelines continue to rely on this failed heuristic, and that the resulting failure modes are not random but predictably categorized into distinct localization, control, and externality breaks. The inclusion of a cross-operator comparison (SAE features vs H-neurons) elevates this work far beyond a simple replication of early probing critiques.

**Objection 2: "You are mixing apples and oranges across operators and tasks."**

*The Rebuttal:* This perceived "apples-to-oranges" mismatch is precisely the core phenomenon under investigation. The paper posits that researchers routinely equate "matched detection" with "matched steerability," systematically ignoring the realities of operator mismatch. By rigorously standardizing the evaluation surface (FaithEval) and the readout quality, the paper isolates the intervention operator as the independent variable directly causing the control break, proving that target-selection heuristics are blind to operational mechanics.

**Objection 3: "Your positive H-neuron result undercuts your own thesis."** *The Rebuttal:* Positive counterexamples do not invalidate a claim of *heuristic unreliability*. The thesis is not that steering is impossible; it is that readouts are an insufficient proxy for control. The fact that H-neurons can be causally steered to reduce hallucination 3 while matched SAE features completely fail on the exact same task perfectly validates the argument that readout quality alone cannot serve as a universal selection heuristic.

**Objection 4: "Your selector comparison is caveated by missing controls."**

*The Rebuttal:* The methodology for selecting targets (e.g., D7-style causal-versus-probe selectors) may introduce confounds based on regularization artifacts or ![][image4] ranking fragility. However, the flagship paper treats this selector evidence strictly as supporting material, not the headline claim. By anchoring the core argument in three deep, distinct case studies spanning the Measurement, Control, and Externality stages, the integrated thesis remains highly robust even if specific selector artifacts prove sensitive to hyperparameter tuning.

**Objection 5: "This is only tested on a single model (Gemma-3-4B-IT)."** *The Rebuttal:* While the experiments are localized to Gemma-3-4B-IT 2 to provide a highly controlled, multimodal-capable testbed, the literature confirms that the phenomena observed scale across diverse architectures. The risk-calibration dissociation operates in 35B MoE models 1, the knowledge-prediction gap is widespread across model families 4, and H-neuron over-compliance scales predictably from 4B to 70B parameters.3 The mechanisms under audit are fundamental to the Transformer architecture itself.

## **13\. Paper-Ready Outputs**

**Introductory Framing Paragraph (Flagship Paper):** While the capacity to detect and decode high-level features within the latent space of Large Language Models has advanced rapidly, the assumption that high-quality readouts automatically yield effective intervention targets remains a critical vulnerability in representation engineering. Recent work has firmly established a correlation-causation dissociation, proving that predictive vectors often lack causal efficacy.1 However, the field lacks a systemic framework to diagnose exactly *where* and *why* these heuristic pipelines fail. In this work, we introduce a four-stage diagnostic scaffold—Measurement, Localization, Control, and Externality—to systematically audit target selection in Gemma-3-4B-IT. We demonstrate that even when localization signals are perfectly matched (e.g., between SAE features and H-neurons), the choice of intervention operator dictates severe control breaks. Furthermore, we show that forcing benchmark-local control on multiple-choice surfaces actively induces catastrophic externalities, such as confident wrong-entity substitution, during open-ended factual generation. Detection is not enough; it is merely an unreliable heuristic that routinely masks multi-stage operational failures.

**Related Work Paragraph (Flagship Paper):** Our work intersects with recent advancements in mechanistic interpretability and activation steering, moving beyond early critiques of linear probing to audit active representation engineering pipelines. Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) and heuristic neuron targeting, such as the isolation of pre-training inherent H-neurons 3, have provided highly precise localization tools. Yet, the translation from localization to control remains deeply fragile, as evidenced by recent findings in 35B MoE models where highly accurate SAE-decoded probes yield zero causal steering efficacy.1 Furthermore, our investigation of steering externalities builds upon foundational observations of surface mismatch, such as the divergence between generative truthfulness and multiple-choice accuracy observed in Inference-Time Intervention 5, and the geometrically distinct knowledge-prediction gap identified in multiple-choice evaluations.4

**Related Work Paragraph (Companion Measurement Note):** The validity of activation steering and safety interventions is fundamentally bounded by the integrity of the evaluation surface. Recent literature highlights severe vulnerabilities in automated assessment protocols that can inadvertently reverse scientific conclusions. The widespread reliance on LLM-as-a-judge frameworks introduces deep, systematic scoring artifacts, where judges frequently reward superficial linguistic formatting or disclaimer-heavy outputs rather than genuine behavioral compliance.6 Concurrently, sequence length and context saturation create truncation artifacts; models under high intrinsic load may exhibit complete compliance failure purely due to generative exhaustion.7 Finally, the reliance on binary classification for safety evaluations masks nuanced, partial compliance, necessitating the adoption of graded scoring to accurately reflect the true efficacy of internal interventions.8
